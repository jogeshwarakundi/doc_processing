Purpose
- Establish a framework for aligning financial incentives between payor(s) and provider(s) to improve quality of care and reduce the total cost of care (TCOC) for a defined population, while protecting quality and access.

Key Definitions
- Total Cost of Care (TCOC): All allowed spending for the attributed patient population across care settings over a defined performance period.
- Shared Savings: A model where providers who reduce TCOC below a benchmark receive a portion of the savings.
- Risk-Sharing (Two-Sided Risk): Providers share in savings and also bear a portion of costs above the benchmark (losses).
- Quality Adjustment Factor (QAF): A multiplier or offset based on performance on agreed quality metrics that modifies shared savings/losses.

Agreement Structure Options
- Upside-only (shared savings only): Provider earns portion of savings if TCOC < benchmark and quality thresholds met; no downside.
- Two-sided risk: Provider receives savings but is also responsible for a portion of losses if TCOC > benchmark.
- Corridor/Performance Threshold: A minimum savings/loss threshold (e.g., ±2–3%) before sharing applies to limit variance.
- Cap and Floor: Limits on maximum shared savings or losses to control financial exposure.

Attribution and Population
- Attribution method: Prospective (assigned at start) or retrospective (based on claims during period). Clearly define inclusion/exclusion (e.g., Medicaid vs Medicare, dual eligibles).
- Risk adjustment: Use an agreed model (e.g., CMS-HCC, clinical risk scores) to adjust benchmarks for case-mix and changes in population acuity.

Benchmarking and Savings Calculation
- Benchmark: Prior historical spending (rolling baseline), regional benchmark, or hybrid; adjust for trend and utilization changes.
- Adjust for one-time events, coding intensity, and population shifts.
- Calculation example:
  - Expected cost (risk-adjusted benchmark) = B
  - Actual cost for period = A
  - Gross savings = max(0, B − A) or gross loss = max(0, A − B)
  - Quality-adjusted shared savings = Gross savings × Sharing rate × Quality Adjustment Factor
- Sharing rate: Negotiated percent (e.g., 50% of savings); different rates may apply across service lines.

Quality Metrics and Performance
- Select a balanced set of metrics tying financial outcomes to care quality, safety, patient experience, and preventative care. Categories:
  - Clinical outcomes (readmissions, HbA1c control, blood pressure control)
  - Utilization measures (ER visit rates, admissions for ambulatory-sensitive conditions)
  - Patient experience (CAHPS scores)
  - Preventive care and care coordination measures
- Define measurement specifications, data sources, reporting cadence, thresholds for minimum acceptable performance, and how metric performance modifies shared savings (bonus/penalty or QAF).
- Consider phased approach: start with process and utilization measures and add outcomes over time.

Data, Reporting, and Transparency
- Regular reporting cadence (monthly/quarterly) with access to claims, encounter, and clinical data.
- Standard data formats, KPIs dashboard, and audit rights for validation.
- Dispute resolution process and reconciliation timeline (e.g., preliminary reports quarterly; final reconciliation annually).

Governance and Operations
- Establish a joint governance committee (executive and clinical) to oversee performance, metric selection, corrective action plans, and appeals.
- Care management, utilization review, and data-sharing protocols to support improvement.
- Performance improvement plans for underperforming metrics with clear milestones.

Financial Flow and Reconciliation
- Timing and method of payments/recoupment: periodic interim distributions vs annual reconciliation.
- Withholds or performance reserves may be held to cover potential losses or quality shortfalls.
- Tax and accounting treatment considerations.

Compliance and Legal Protections
- Address regulatory compliance (Stark Law, Anti-Kickback Statute, state laws), ensure arrangements meet safe-harbor requirements or are structured under value-based exceptions.
- Patient protections: continuity of care, no reduction in access, clear communication about care management.
- Data privacy and security: HIPAA-compliant data exchange and business associate agreements.

Risk Mitigation and Contingencies
- Stop-loss/maximum loss limits, reinsurance options for extreme variance.
- Periodic re-baselining of benchmarks to avoid stale targets.
- Mechanism for addressing major population changes (e.g., epidemics, facility closures).

Termination and Transition
- Define exit triggers (material breach, regulatory change), reconciliation procedures, final audit period, and responsibilities for open claims and patient transitions.

Implementation Roadmap (high-level)
1. Define population, attribution, and data feeds.
2. Agree benchmarks, risk-adjustment method, sharing parameters, and quality metrics.
3. Build reporting and reconciliation processes; governance structure.
4. Pilot period with frequent reviews; refine metrics and calculations.
5. Scale to full performance year, with annual re-evaluation.

Sample contractual language (short)
- “Shared savings shall be determined annually by comparing the risk-adjusted benchmark to actual TCOC for the attributed population. Provider shall be entitled to X% of shared savings only if a Quality Adjustment Factor of at least Y% is achieved across the agreed metric set. In a two-sided model, provider shall be responsible for X% of any excess costs above the benchmark, subject to a loss cap of $Z per performance year and a corridor of ±2%.”

Risks and Considerations
- Upcoding/coding intensity, data lags, and attribution disputes.
- Provider financial strain under rapid downside exposure.
- Need for robust care management infrastructure to achieve improvements.

Periodic reconciliation and true-up processes involve regularly comparing actual utilization or experience to actuarial benchmarks and making adjustments to reflect over- or underutilization. At scheduled intervals (monthly, quarterly, or annually), actuaries and finance teams reconcile utilization data, verifying data quality, applying credibility factors, and quantifying variances from expected costs or volumes. Material deviations trigger a true-up: financial adjustments to reserves, premiums, or vendor payments, accompanied by root-cause analysis to determine whether variance stems from data issues, temporary trends, or structural changes. Governance procedures define thresholds for automatic adjustments, approval workflows, stakeholder communications, and required documentation to ensure transparency and auditability. The process supports regulatory compliance, improves forecasting accuracy, and informs corrective actions such as benefit design changes, pricing updates, or utilization management initiatives to align future experience with actuarial expectations.
